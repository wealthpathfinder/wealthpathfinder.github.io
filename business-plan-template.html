Section 1: Executive Summary Template

The most important sectionâ€”often the only part investors read first.

ðŸ“ What to Include (1-2 pages max)

Write this section LAST, even though it appears first. Summarize the key points from each section.

1. The Hook (1 paragraph)

Template: "[Company Name] is a [type of business] that provides [core product/service] to [target market]. We solve [specific problem] by [unique solution]."

Example (Coffee Shop): "Brew Haven is a specialty coffee shop that provides premium coffee experiences to urban professionals in downtown Seattle. We solve the lack of quality, convenient coffee options by offering ethically sourced beans, expert preparation, and a productive workspace environment."

2. Market Opportunity (1 paragraph)

Template: "The [industry] market is valued at $[size] and growing at [growth rate] annually. Our target segment represents $[segment size] with [key trend] creating demand for our solution."

Example: "The specialty coffee market is valued at $48 billion and growing at 8.3% annually. Our target segment of urban professionals in downtown Seattle represents a $15 million opportunity, with remote work trends increasing demand for quality third spaces."

3. Business Model (1 paragraph)

Template: "We generate revenue through [primary revenue stream] at [average price point]. Additional revenue comes from [secondary streams]. Our cost structure is primarily [main costs] with target gross margin of [margin]%."

Example: "We generate revenue through direct coffee sales at $4.50 average ticket. Additional revenue comes from pastry sales, merchandise, and monthly coffee subscriptions. Our cost structure is primarily coffee beans and labor with target gross margin of 65%."

4. Traction/Progress (1 paragraph)

Template: "To date, we have [achievements]. Our pilot/test showed [results]. We have [number] customers/clients with [satisfaction metric]."

Example: "To date, we have secured our ideal location, completed build-out plans, and tested our concept through pop-up events attracting 200+ daily customers. Our pilot showed 45% repeat customer rate and 4.8/5 satisfaction score."

5. Team (1 paragraph)

Template: "Our team includes [founder name] with [relevant experience], [key hire] with [expertise], and advisors with [industry connections]."

Example: "Our team includes founder Sarah Chen with 10 years of Starbucks management experience, head barista Marco Rossi with national latte art championships, and advisor David Park who successfully scaled three coffee chains."

6. Funding Needs & Use (1 paragraph)

Template: "We are seeking $[amount] in [type of funding] to achieve [specific milestones]. Funds will be used for [breakdown: 40% equipment, 30% marketing, etc.]."

Example: "We are seeking $150,000 in seed funding to achieve breakeven within 12 months. Funds will be used for equipment (40%), initial inventory (20%), marketing (25%), and working capital (15%)."

7. Financial Projections (1 paragraph)

Template: "We project Year 1 revenue of $[amount], growing to $[amount] by Year 3. We expect to achieve profitability in [timeframe] and target [ROI/return] for investors."

Example: "We project Year 1 revenue of $350,000, growing to $850,000 by Year 3. We expect to achieve profitability in Month 14 and target 4x return for investors within 5 years."

ðŸ’¡ Financial Projection Tips

Be Conservative

Assume everything will take longer and cost more than expected. Underpromise, overdeliver.

Show Monthly Detail for Year 1

Investors want to see the ramp-up period month by month.

Include Assumptions

Every number should have a clear assumption behind it. "We assume 2% conversion from website traffic."

Plan for the Unexpected

Include a contingency line item (10-15% of total costs).

Common Business Plan Mistakes to Avoid

ðŸš« 1. Unrealistic Financial Projections

The Mistake: Projecting million-dollar revenues in Year 1 with no basis in reality.

The Fix: Start with industry benchmarks, be conservative, show monthly ramp-up, include detailed assumptions.

ðŸš« 2. No Clear Target Market

The Mistake: "Everyone is our customer" or vague descriptions like "small businesses."

The Fix: Define specific customer segments with demographics, psychographics, pain points, and buying behaviors.

ðŸš« 3. Ignoring Competition

The Mistake: "We have no competition" or failing to analyze competitors' strengths and weaknesses.

The Fix: Detailed competitive analysis showing how you're different and better. Acknowledge competitors' strengths.

ðŸš« 4. Weak Team Section

The Mistake: Just listing names without explaining why this team can execute the plan.

The Fix: Highlight relevant experience, past successes, and how skills complement each other. Address gaps honestly.

ðŸš« 5. Too Much Jargon

The Mistake: Using industry buzzwords without explaining what they mean for the business.

The Fix: Write for a smart 15-year-old. If you must use technical terms, include brief explanations.

ðŸš« 6. No Implementation Plan

The Mistake: Great ideas but no clear plan for who does what, when, and with what resources.

The Fix: Include timelines, milestones, responsibilities, and resource requirements for key activities.

ðŸš« 7. Forgetting the Appendix

The Mistake: No supporting documents to back up claims.

The Fix: Include resumes, product photos, market research data, letters of intent, patents, etc.

ðŸš« 8. Typos and Formatting Errors

The Mistake: Spelling errors, inconsistent formatting, poor layout.

The Fix: Proofread multiple times, use consistent headings, include table of contents, get fresh eyes to review.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should my business plan be?

It depends on your purpose:

  • Internal use only: 1-10 pages
  • Bank loan for existing business: 15-25 pages
  • VC funding for startup: 20-40 pages
  • Simple side business: 1 page (One-Page Business Plan)

Quality over quantity: A concise, well-researched 15-page plan is better than a vague 40-page plan.

How detailed should financial projections be?

Include at minimum:

  1. Startup costs: One-time expenses to launch
  2. Monthly operating expenses: Fixed and variable costs
  3. Sales forecast: Monthly for Year 1, quarterly for Years 2-3
  4. Cash flow statement: When money comes in and goes out
  5. Profit & loss statement: Shows profitability
  6. Break-even analysis: When you'll cover costs

Key: All numbers should have clear assumptions explained in footnotes or a separate assumptions page.

Should I hire someone to write my business plan?

Consider these factors:

âœ… Do It Yourself If:
  • You know your business intimately
  • You're bootstrapping or seeking <$50K
  • You want to deeply understand every aspect
  • You have writing/research skills
ðŸ’° Hire a Professional If:
  • Seeking significant funding (>$100K)
  • Weak at writing/financial analysis
  • Time is more valuable than money
  • Need investor-ready polish

Hybrid approach: Write first draft yourself, then hire a professional to polish and add financial rigor.

How often should I update my business plan?

Regular review schedule:

  • Monthly: Compare actual vs. projected financials
  • Quarterly: Review progress on key milestones
  • Annually: Complete review and update of entire plan
  • When seeking funding: Always update before presenting
  • When strategy changes: Major pivots require plan updates

Remember: A business plan is a living document, not something you write once and forget.

What's the difference between a business plan and a pitch deck?

Business Plan: Comprehensive document (10-40 pages) with detailed analysis, financials, and implementation plans. Used for due diligence.

Pitch Deck: Presentation (10-15 slides) highlighting key points for initial meetings. Visual, concise, tells a compelling story.

Rule of thumb: Create a pitch deck first for initial interest, then provide business plan for serious investors during due diligence.

ðŸš€ Start Today, Not Tomorrow

The biggest mistake entrepreneurs make is waiting for the "perfect time" to write their business plan. Start today with just 30 minutes. Open a document and answer these three questions:

1. What problem am I solving?

Be specific. Not "coffee" but "lack of quality coffee options for downtown professionals who value ethically sourced beans and productive work environments."

2. Who will pay me to solve it?

Describe your ideal customer in detail: demographics, behaviors, pain points, willingness to pay.

3. How will I reach them?

List 3-5 specific channels where you'll find and convert customers.

That's it. You've started your business plan. Continue with 30 minutes daily, and in 30 days, you'll have a complete, professional plan that dramatically increases your chances of success.
