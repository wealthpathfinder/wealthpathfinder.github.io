When to Ask for a Raise: The 5 Best Timing Scenarios

1. After a Major Achievement

95% Success Rate

Perfect Timing: 2-4 weeks after completing a successful project, exceeding targets, or receiving positive recognition.

Example: After leading a project that generated $500K in new revenue, wait for the quarterly results to be announced, then schedule your meeting.
Preparation: Document specific metrics, gather praise emails, quantify your contribution.

2. During Annual Review Period

80% Success Rate

Perfect Timing: 1-2 weeks before your formal review meeting.

Example: If reviews happen in March, schedule a pre-review conversation in late February to set expectations.
Preparation: Complete a self-assessment highlighting achievements, gather market salary data.

3. After Taking on Additional Responsibilities

70% Success Rate

Perfect Timing: 3-6 months into new responsibilities, once you've demonstrated competence.

Example: After successfully managing a team for 4 months following a colleague's departure.
Preparation: Document how you've exceeded in the new role, research salary for the expanded position.

4. Company Financial Success

60% Success Rate

Perfect Timing: After positive earnings reports, successful funding rounds, or major contract wins.

Example: Following announcement of 20% quarterly growth or successful Series B funding.
Preparation: Connect your contributions to company success, emphasize future potential.

5. Market Rate Adjustment

40% Success Rate

Timing: When market data shows you're significantly underpaid (20%+ below market).

Example: Research shows your position pays $85K average, you earn $65K.
Preparation: Extensive market research, compelling data presentation, be prepared for pushback.

âš  When NOT to Ask for a Raise

  • During company layoffs or financial struggles (wait 3-6 months after recovery)
  • Within first 6 months of employment (exceptions for exceptional circumstances)
  • After poor performance reviews (address performance first)
  • During your manager's personal crisis (bad timing shows poor judgment)
  • When you have no new accomplishments (same work = same pay in most companies)

Exact Negotiation Scripts (Word-for-Word)

Scenario 1: Annual Review Meeting

You: "Thank you for taking the time to meet with me today. I've really enjoyed my work this past year, particularly [mention 1-2 key achievements]. Based on my contributions and market research, I was hoping to discuss my compensation."
Manager: "Sure, what are you thinking?"
You: "Based on my research, the market rate for someone with my experience and responsibilities is between $[range]. Given my contributions this year, including [specific achievement 1] and [specific achievement 2], I believe an adjustment to $[specific amount] would be appropriate. What are your thoughts?"
Key Elements:
  • Start with gratitude and positivity
  • Reference specific achievements
  • Present data-backed range
  • End with open question

Scenario 2: After Major Project Success

You: "I'm really proud of what our team accomplished with [Project Name]. The results exceeded expectations by [percentage/number], and I wanted to discuss how this success might be reflected in my compensation going forward."
Manager: "The project did go well. What did you have in mind?"
You: "Given that I led [specific aspect] which contributed directly to the $[amount] in additional revenue, and considering my expanded responsibilities, I believe an increase to $[amount] would recognize this contribution. This aligns with market rates for similar roles with these responsibilities."

Scenario 3: Countering a Low Offer

Manager: "We can offer a 3% increase this year."
You: "I appreciate that offer. Based on my research, the standard increase for high performers in our industry is typically 5-7%. Given my [specific achievement] that resulted in [quantifiable result], I was hoping we could discuss something closer to 7%. Does that seem reasonable based on my contributions?"
Key Elements:
  • Acknowledge their offer first
  • Present industry standards
  • Reconnect to your value
  • Make it a collaborative discussion

Scenario 4: The Silent Pause Technique

You: "Based on my research and contributions, I believe $[amount] would be appropriate."
Manager: "[Silence or non-committal response]"
You: [Remain silent for 5-7 seconds. Don't speak first.]
Manager: "Well, that's higher than we typically do for this role..."
You: "I understand. Could you help me understand the typical range for someone with my responsibilities and achievements?"
Psychology:

The first person to speak after stating their position loses leverage. Comfort with silence is a powerful negotiation tool.

Perfect Timing Strategies

1. The 2-Week Pre-Review Strategy

2 weeks before review: Schedule meeting to discuss compensation
1 week before review: Send your value document
Review meeting: Formal discussion with manager prepared
Benefits:
  • Sets expectations before formal process
  • Gives manager time to advocate for you
  • Avoids surprise discussions
  • Shows strategic thinking

2. The Quarterly Success Strategy

Q1: Set clear goals with metrics
Q2: Document achievements mid-way
Q3: Schedule raise discussion
Q4: Implement new salary
Benefits:
  • Aligns with business cycles
  • Builds case over time
  • Demonstrates consistent performance
  • Fits budget planning cycles

3. The Project-Based Strategy

Project start: Document baseline metrics
During project: Track contributions and wins
Project completion: Quantify success metrics
2-4 weeks after: Schedule compensation discussion
Benefits:
  • Success is fresh in everyone's mind
  • Clear cause-and-effect relationship
  • Emotional momentum from success
  • Hard to argue with recent results

Frequently Asked Questions

What percentage raise should I ask for?

Typical ranges:

  • Cost of living adjustment: 3-4% (matches inflation)
  • Merit increase for good performance: 5-7%
  • Promotion or significant responsibility increase: 10-15%
    • <
  • Market adjustment (if underpaid): 15-25%
  • Extraordinary performance or critical role: 20%+

Rule of thumb: Ask for 5-10% more than your target to leave room for negotiation.

What if they say no to a raise?

Respond with: "I understand. Could we discuss what specific achievements or milestones would warrant a salary increase in the future?" Then:

  1. Get specific goals: "What would success look like over the next 6 months?"
  2. Set review timeline: "When would be a good time to revisit this conversation?"
  3. Explore alternatives: "If salary isn't possible now, could we discuss bonus potential, additional vacation, professional development budget, or remote work flexibility?"
  4. Document everything: Send a follow-up email summarizing the conversation and agreed-upon next steps.

Should I mention another job offer during negotiation?

Generally no, unless:

  • You're genuinely prepared to leave
  • The offer is significantly better (25%+ increase)
  • You have a strong relationship with your manager
  • You frame it as wanting to stay but needing fair compensation

Better approach: Use market data rather than specific offers. Say "market research shows..." rather than "I have an offer for..."

How do I negotiate salary in a remote position?

Key strategies for remote negotiations:

  1. Use video calls: Never negotiate solely via email or chat
  2. Research location-based salaries: Remote doesn't always mean location-agnostic pay
  3. Highlight remote-specific value: Increased productivity, availability across time zones, cost savings to company
  4. Consider total compensation: Home office stipend, co-working membership, tech budget
  5. Document communication clearly: Follow up video calls with written summaries
