What is a Balance Transfer?

A balance transfer moves debt from one or more high-interest credit cards to a new card with a 0% or low introductory APR. Here's how it works:

Step 1: Apply for a Balance Transfer Card

Choose a card with a long 0% introductory period (15-21 months is best). Your credit score needs to be good to excellent (670+).

Step 2: Initiate the Transfer

After approval, provide information about your existing debts. The new card issuer pays off your old cards directly.

Step 3: Pay 0% Interest During Intro Period

For the next 12-21 months, you pay 0% interest on the transferred balance (regular APR applies to new purchases).

Step 4: Pay Down Debt Faster

Since 100% of your payment goes toward principal (no interest), you can pay off debt much faster.

ðŸ’¼ The Math That Makes Balance Transfers Powerful

With a 24% APR credit card, only about 50% of your minimum payment goes toward principal. With a 0% balance transfer, 100% goes toward principal. This means you can pay off debt 2x faster with the same monthly payment.

  • Longest: 18-21 months

  • Transfer limits: Usually equal to credit limit
  • Same issuer transfers: Often not allowed

How to Apply & Get Approved for Balance Transfer Cards

Step 1: Check Your Credit Score First

Most balance transfer cards require good to excellent credit (670+). Check your score for free through your bank or credit card issuer before applying.

Step 2: Calculate Your Debt-to-Credit Ratio

Keep utilization below 30% on existing cards before applying. Pay down balances if possible to improve approval odds.

Step 3: Choose the Right Card

Match the card to your needs: Longest 0% period vs lowest fee vs rewards. Don't just apply for the first offer you see.

Step 4: Gather Required Information

You'll need: Social Security number, annual income, employment information, monthly housing payment, existing card information for transfers.

Step 5: Apply Online for Instant Decision

Most applications get instant approval/denial. If pending, call reconsideration line: 888-245-0625 (Citi), 800-432-1000 (Bank of America), 888-675-1461 (Chase).

Step 6: Initiate Transfer Immediately

Most cards require transfers within 30-60 days of account opening. Do it right away to maximize 0% period.

ðŸ’¼ The "Reconsideration Line" Secret

If you're denied or get a low credit limit, call the reconsideration line. Be prepared to explain: 1) Why you want the card, 2) How you'll pay the balance, 3) Your income/employment stability. They can sometimes overturn denials or increase limits.

7 Balance Transfer Mistakes to Avoid

1. Making New Purchases on the Card

Mistake: Using the balance transfer card for new purchases.

Why it's bad: Payments typically go to lowest APR balance first (0% transfers), so new purchases at high APR accrue interest immediately.

Solution: Don't use the card for purchases. Cut it up or freeze it in ice.

2. Missing a Payment

Mistake: Being even one day late on a payment.

Why it's bad: Most cards will void the 0% offer entirely if you're late. You'll owe back interest on the entire balance.

Solution: Set up autopay for at least the minimum payment.

3. Not Calculating the Break-Even Point

Mistake: Transferring without doing the math.

Why it's bad: The transfer fee might cost more than the interest you'd pay.

Solution: Use our calculator above. Generally, need at least 6+ months of interest savings to outweigh fee.

4. Closing Old Accounts Immediately

Mistake: Closing credit cards after transferring balances.

Why it's bad: Hurts your credit utilization ratio and average account age.

Solution: Keep old accounts open but don't use them. Monitor for fraud.

5. Using Cards You Just Paid Off

Mistake: Running up balances on cards you just transferred from.

Why it's bad: You end up with even more debt than before.

Solution: Cut up or freeze old cards. Change spending habits.

6. Not Having a Payoff Plan

Mistake: Getting the card without a repayment plan.

Why it's bad: 0% period ends, then high APR kicks in on remaining balance.

Solution: Divide balance by (intro months minus 1). That's your minimum monthly payment.

7. Transferring Between Same-Issuer Cards

Mistake: Trying to transfer from one Chase card to another Chase card.

Why it's bad: Most issuers don't allow transfers between their own cards.

Solution: Check issuer rules first. Usually need to transfer to a different issuer's card.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a balance transfer affect my credit score?

Short-term: Hard inquiry (-5 points) and new account (average age decreases). Long-term: Lower credit utilization improves score significantly. Overall, your score may dip 10-20 points initially but should recover and improve within 3-6 months as you pay down debt and utilization drops.

Can I do a balance transfer from a personal loan or student loan?

Generally, no. Most credit cards only allow balance transfers from other credit cards. Some may allow transfers from store cards or gas cards. Personal loans, student loans, auto loans, and mortgages typically cannot be transferred to credit cards. Check with the specific card issuer for their policies.

What happens if I don't pay off the balance during the 0% period?

The remaining balance will start accruing interest at the regular APR, which is typically 18-29%. Some cards may also charge deferred interest (rare with major issuers), meaning you owe all the interest that would have accrued during the 0% period. Always read the terms carefully.

Can I do multiple balance transfers to the same card?

Usually yes, but with limitations. Most cards allow additional transfers during the first 60-90 days after account opening. However, each transfer typically has its own fee, and the 0% period for each transfer usually starts from the account opening date (not the transfer date). Check your card's specific terms.
