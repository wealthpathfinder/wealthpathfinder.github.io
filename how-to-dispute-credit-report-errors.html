ðŸ’¼ Legal Protection: The Fair Credit Reporting Act

Under the FCRA, credit bureaus MUST investigate disputes within 30 days (45 if you send additional information). If they cannot verify the information, they MUST remove it. This federal law is your most powerful tool for fixing credit report errors.

8 Most Common Credit Report Errors to Look For

1. Incorrect Personal Information

Wrong name, address, Social Security number, or employment history. Mixed files with someone who has a similar name.

  • Impact: Minor to severe
  • Fix: Dispute with all bureaus
  • Common cause: Data entry errors

2. Accounts That Aren't Yours

Accounts opened by identity thieves or belonging to family members with similar names.

  • Impact: Severe
  • Fix: Immediate dispute + fraud alert
  • Warning sign: Unknown credit inquiries

3. Incorrect Account Status

Accounts shown as open when closed, or vice versa. Wrong payment status (current vs delinquent).

  • Impact: Moderate to severe
  • Fix: Dispute with bureau and creditor
  • Proof needed: Account statements

4. Duplicate Accounts

Same account listed multiple times, especially after debt is sold to collections.

  • Impact: Moderate
  • Fix: Dispute duplicates
  • Look for: Same account numbers

5. Outdated Negative Information

Bankruptcies older than 10 years, late payments older than 7 years, collections past 7-year limit.

  • Impact: Moderate to severe
  • Fix: Dispute for removal
  • FCRA rule: 7-10 year limits

6. Wrong Account Balances/Limits

Incorrect credit limits, balances, or payment amounts affecting credit utilization ratio.

  • Impact: Moderate
  • Fix: Provide current statements
  • Check: Recent statements vs report

7. Incorrect Payment History

Late payments you actually paid on time. Missed payments during deferment periods.

  • Impact: Severe
  • Fix: Provide payment proof
  • Evidence: Bank statements, receipts

8. Fraudulent Credit Inquiries

Hard inquiries you didn't authorize (potential identity theft).

  • Impact: Minor to moderate
  • Fix: Dispute + fraud alert
  • Note: Soft inquiries don't affect score

How to Review Your Credit Reports: Line-by-Line Checklist

When reviewing your reports, check these sections carefully:

Section 1: Personal Information

Check: Name (including misspellings), current and previous addresses, Social Security number, date of birth, employment history.

What to flag: Addresses where you never lived, employers you never worked for, wrong SSN digits.

Section 2: Accounts (Tradelines)

Check: Every accountâ€”open and closed. Verify account numbers, type of account, open/closed status, balance, credit limit, payment history, account status.

What to flag: Accounts you don't recognize, incorrect balances, accounts showing as open when closed, late payments you made on time.

Section 3: Inquiries

Check: Hard inquiries (affect score) for last 2 years. Soft inquiries (don't affect score).

What to flag: Hard inquiries you didn't authorize, duplicate inquiries from same lender.

Section 4: Public Records & Collections

Check: Bankruptcies (10-year limit), tax liens, civil judgments, collection accounts.

What to flag: Items older than reporting time limits, paid collections still showing as unpaid, duplicate collections.

ðŸ“‹ Document Everything

When you find an error: 1) Circle it on the paper report, 2) Take a screenshot if online, 3) Note the page and item number, 4) Gather supporting documents (statements, payment confirmations, IDs). This documentation is crucial for your dispute.

Online disputes are convenient but: 1) You waive certain legal rights, 2) Can't send physical evidence easily, 3) Limited character counts for explanations, 4) May be routed to automated systems. For complex disputes or when you have documentation, certified mail is better.

Mail Dispute Process (Most Effective Method)

Disputing by certified mail creates a paper trail and preserves all your legal rights. Here's exactly how:

Step 1: Write Your Dispute Letter

Use our template below. Include: Your information, report confirmation number, item details, reason for dispute, requested action, list of enclosures.

Step 2: Gather Supporting Documents

Include: Copy of credit report with error circled, government-issued ID, proof of address, account statements, payment receipts, police report (if identity theft).

Step 3: Make Copies of Everything

Keep complete copies of your letter and all documents. You'll need these for follow-up or if you need to escalate.

Step 4: Send via Certified Mail

Use USPS Certified Mail with Return Receipt Requested. This provides proof of delivery and the date received.

Step 5: Send to All Three Bureaus

Send separate disputes to each bureau that shows the error. They don't share dispute information.

Step 6: Send to Data Furnisher

Also send dispute to the original creditor (data furnisher). They must investigate too under FCRA Section 623.

What to Do After Filing a Dispute

Step 1: Track the 30/45-Day Timeline

Credit bureaus have 30 days to investigate (45 if you provide additional information after initial dispute). Mark your calendar.

Step 2: Save All Correspondence

Keep copies of everything: your dispute, their response, any intermediate communications.

Step 3: Review the Results

You'll receive results by mail (and sometimes email). Three possible outcomes:

  • Deleted/Corrected: Success! Error fixed.
  • Verified: Bureau says item is accurate.
  • Investigation Incomplete: Need more time.

Step 4: Request Method of Verification

If they say an item is "verified," send a Method of Verification letter asking how they verified it.

Step 5: Get Your Updated Report

If items are changed, you're entitled to a free updated report. Request it to confirm corrections.

Step 6: Check All Three Reports

Even if one bureau fixes an error, the others may still show it. Dispute with each bureau separately.

How to Escalate if Your Dispute is Denied

If the credit bureau says an error is "verified," don't give up. Here are your escalation options:

1. Request Method of Verification

Send a letter demanding to know exactly how they verified the information. They must provide procedures used.

  • Timing: Within 30 days of dispute results
  • Template: "Pursuant to FCRA Â§ 611(a)(7), please provide the method of verification..."
  • Outcome: Often leads to deletion if they can't provide adequate verification

2. File CFPB Complaint

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau can pressure bureaus and creditors to fix errors.

  • Website: consumerfinance.gov/complaint
  • Success rate: High - companies respond within 15 days
  • Free: No cost to you
  • Track: Online tracking of complaint status

3. Contact Your State Attorney General

State AGs have consumer protection divisions that handle credit reporting complaints.

  • Find yours: naag.org/find-my-ag
  • Effective: Especially for pattern of complaints
  • Additional: Some states have stronger credit laws than federal

4. Add a Consumer Statement

If you can't get an error removed, add a 100-word statement explaining your side.

  • Length: 100 words maximum
  • Cost: Free to add
  • Impact: Lenders see it when reviewing your report
  • Limitation: Doesn't affect your credit score

5. Consult a Consumer Attorney

If errors are causing significant harm, consult an FCRA attorney. Many work on contingency.

  • When: Denied major loan, wrong information persists
  • Cost: Often free consultation, contingency fee if they take case
  • Damages: Can sue for actual damages + $100-$1000 per violation

6. Direct Dispute with Creditor

Under FCRA Section 623, you can dispute directly with the creditor who furnished the data.

  • Address: Send to their legal/consumer affairs department
  • Timing: They have 30 days to investigate
  • Result: If they find error, must notify all bureaus

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a credit dispute take?

By law, credit bureaus have 30 days to investigate most disputes (45 days if you provide additional information after filing). Online disputes may be resolved in 10-14 days, while mail disputes take the full 30 days plus mailing time.

Will disputing hurt my credit score?

No. Disputing errors does not affect your credit score. The dispute process itself is not reported to lenders. However, if errors are corrected and negative items are removed, your score will likely improve.

What if all three credit reports show different information?

This is common since bureaus don't share data. You must dispute with each bureau separately. What's on one report won't automatically be on others. Some creditors report to all three, some to only one or two.

Can I pay someone to fix my credit report?

You can, but it's usually unnecessary. Credit repair companies charge $50-$150/month and do exactly what this guide teaches you to do for free. The only thing they can do that you can't is provide legal representation if needed. Most errors can be fixed yourself for free.
